About the time anchor

Why the long-cycle dates stay reliable far back in time

This is a Qi Men Dun Jia chart through and through: the plates, stems, nine stars, eight doors and eight deities are all computed by the standard QMDJ method. Nothing from Tai Yi enters the content of the chart.

The only place an outside reference is used is the dating of the long cycles (the 540-year palace and its sub-cycles). To anchor that count far back in time you need a stable origin epoch; rather than extrapolating a sexagenary count Bazi-style — which accrues drift and ambiguity over thousands of years — the system adopts an epoch that is already fixed by an authoritative calendar: the 上元 of the 太乙金鏡 tradition (the Tang-dynasty “Golden Mirror”), namely 1,936,557 BC.

That epoch is chosen because it falls exactly on a boundary of the cyclic lattice (4860×399 = 92,340×21 years before the 2584 AD reset): it gives a coherent, checkable zero-point, not a guess. In short, Tai Yi is used as a reference calendar for dating the cycles, not as a reading technique.

To be clear, so there is no confusion: Tai Yi and Qi Men are not being merged. The chart stays entirely QMDJ; the 金鏡 is only the ruler used to measure the years. The Metonic coherence (19-year cycle) is a phase property of the calendrical counter, not an astronomical claim.
